Charlotte Maxeke

7 April 1871 - 16 October 1939

Here is a time line of Charlotte Maxeke's life

1871: Charlotte Maxeke is born on April 7th in Ramokgopa, South Africa, in what is now Limpopo province.


1880s-1890s: Maxeke receives education at a missionary school, where she is one of the first Black South Africans to receive formal education.


1891: Maxeke helps co-found the Bantu Women's League, an organization dedicated to promoting women's rights and addressing the social and economic challenges faced by Black women in South Africa.


1896: Maxeke becomes the first Black South African woman to receive a university degree when she graduates from Wilberforce University in Ohio, United States.


1897: Maxeke marries Marshall Maxeke, a fellow South African student at Wilberforce, and they return to South Africa.


1913: Inspired by the 1913 Women's Suffrage March in America, Maxeke leads 10,000 women in a protest march against discriminatory laws affecting Black women in South Africa, known as the "March of the Women."


1918: Founding member of the Bantu Women's League and wife of the African National Congress (ANC) president, Maxeke plays an active role in promoting political and human rights activism, especially through her involvement in various women's organizations.


1920: Maxeke establishes the Wilberforce Institute in Evaton, South Africa, to provide education and vocational training for Black women.


1928: Maxeke leads a delegation of South African women to the Women's International League for Peace and Freedom conference in Switzerland, advocating for peace and equal rights for all women.


1930s: Maxeke continues her involvement in political and social activism, speaking out against the pass laws, racial discrimination, and advocating for improved living conditions in Black townships.


1939: Charlotte Maxeke passes away on October 16th, leaving behind a legacy as a pioneering activist, educator, and champion for women's rights in South Africa and beyond.


2021: Maxeke's contributions are widely recognized, with several institutions, including the University of the Witwatersrand in Johannesburg, being named after her. She is celebrated as an icon of the struggle for freedom and equality in South Africa.
